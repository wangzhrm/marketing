Porter's Five Forces Model

A framework for analyzing a company's competitive environment and determining the intensity of competition in an industry

Porter's Five Forces is a model developed by Michael E. Porter of Harvard Business School in 1979. The five forces framework helps analyze the level of competition and attractiveness of an industry.

Understanding these forces helps organizations develop competitive strategies by identifying where power lies in a business situation.

Threat of New Entrants

Factors to Consider:

  • Barriers to entry (capital requirements, regulations)
  • Economies of scale
  • Product differentiation
  • Brand equity and customer loyalty
  • Access to distribution channels
  • Government policies and licensing
  • Switching costs for customers
  • Expected retaliation from existing firms

Bargaining Power of Buyers

Factors to Consider:

  • Number of buyers relative to sellers
  • Size of each order
  • Switching costs for buyers
  • Price sensitivity
  • Availability of substitute products
  • Buyer's information availability
  • Ability to backward integrate
  • Buyer's profitability

Bargaining Power of Suppliers

Factors to Consider:

  • Number of suppliers relative to competitors
  • Uniqueness of supplier's product or service
  • Switching costs for firms in the industry
  • Presence of substitute inputs
  • Supplier's ability to forward integrate
  • Importance of volume to supplier
  • Cost of inputs relative to selling price
  • Supplier concentration

Threat of Substitute Products

Factors to Consider:

  • Number of substitute products available
  • Relative price performance of substitutes
  • Switching costs for customers
  • Buyer propensity to substitute
  • Perceived level of product differentiation
  • Availability of close substitutes
  • Substitute product quality
  • Marketing efforts of substitute products

Intensity of Competitive Rivalry

Factors to Consider:

  • Number of competitors
  • Industry growth rate
  • Fixed costs versus value added
  • Intermittent overcapacity
  • Product differences
  • Brand identity
  • Switching costs
  • Exit barriers
  • Diversity of competitors
  • Corporate stakes
  • Level of advertising expense
